Samuel Insull

Samuel Insull in 1920

Timeline of his life

1859
Samuel is born in London.
1873
Becomes an apprentice clerk for various local businesses.
1878
Gets a new job as the secretary for the London representative of Thomas Edison's telephone companies.
1881
Emigrates to the United States and continues working for Edison, climbing the ranks inside his company.
1892
Is offered the post of second vice president at General Electric, is unhappy that he wasn't named president.
1894
Discovers the concept of the electric meter while on a Christmas visit to Brighton, England.
1897
Introduces the meter to customers of his electricity in Chicago. Instead of paying electricity bill that depend solely on the number of lightbulbs installed, customers now pay for the energy they use.
This helps make electric lighting economically viable for many more customers.
1900-1929
Continues building a financial empire centered on utilities. During that time, he comes up with what will be his biggest legacy: the concept of natural monopolies. Insull realizes that unlike other markets, it is inefficient to have multiple (sometimes dozens) electricity providers, with their own power plants, grids and infrastructure compete in the same markets.

Instead, he proposes that electricity markets should be regulated, with a single company operating within limits fixed by a regulator. This way, the company benefits from the scale of serving a large number of customers, is incentivized to reduce costs of electricity, and is prevented from abusing its monopoly by the regulator, which sets a fair limit to its profits.

Nowadays, this concept is extremely common for private operators of critical infrastructure such as providing water, highways or airports. By the 1920s, about half of the american states were regulating electricity providers in this manner.
1929
His holding company collapses during the Great Depression, he loses millions and flees to France.
1934
Is extradited back to the United States by Turkey on mail fraud and antitrust charges but is ultimately found not guilty by the jury after they deliberate for only five minutes. To avoid making their verdict look suspicious, the jurors hold a birthday party for one of them and even order cake and coffee, before finally re-entering the courtroom to announce their decision.
1938
Dies of a heart attack at the Place de la Concorde subway station in Paris. He had debts totaling $14,000,000.
